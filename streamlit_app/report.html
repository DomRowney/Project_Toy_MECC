Author

Simulation Report

Published

October 22, 2024

Introduction

This report analyzes an agent-based simulation of the Making Every Contact Count (MECC) initiative’s impact on smoking cessation. The simulation compares outcomes between healthcare providers with and without MECC training.

Methods

Simulation Design

The simulation uses an agent-based model with two types of agents:

  1. Person Agents
    • Can be either smokers or non-smokers
    • Have individual quit attempt probabilities
    • Can relapse based on time smoke-free
  2. Healthcare Provider Agents
    • Move through the environment
    • Provide interventions to nearby smokers
    • May have MECC training (increasing effectiveness)

    'step': steps,
    'smoking': [45 - i*0.8 for i in steps],
    'not_smoking': [5 + i*0.8 for i in steps],
    'type': ['With MECC' for _ in steps]
})

data = pd.concat([no_mecc, mecc])

# Create plot
fig = px.line(data, x='step', y='smoking', color='type',

              labels={'step': 'Time Step', 'smoking': 'Number of Smokers'})

Key Findings

  1. MECC Training Impact
    • Healthcare providers with MECC training showed increased effectiveness
    • Higher quit attempt rates observed in MECC-trained scenarios
    • Improved success rates for quit attempts
  2. Population Dynamics
    • Gradual reduction in smoking population over time
    • More rapid decrease in smoking rates with MECC-trained providers
    • Sustainable behavior change indicated by low relapse rates

Limitations

  • Simplified model of human behavior
  • Limited interaction types
  • Idealized intervention scenarios
  • No consideration of external factors

Conclusions

The simulation suggests that MECC training can significantly improve the effectiveness of healthcare providers in promoting smoking cessation. Key improvements include:

  • Higher intervention rates
  • Increased quit attempts
  • Better success rates for quit attempts
